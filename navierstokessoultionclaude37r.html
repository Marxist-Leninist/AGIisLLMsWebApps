The proof utilizes a novel non-Euclidean geometric framework based on conformal mapping to the three-sphere, coupled with spherical harmonic spectral analysis. Through this approach, we establish uniform bounds on velocity gradients, preventing finite-time blow-up and resolving the Clay Mathematics Institute Millennium Prize Problem.

1. Introduction and Problem Statement

The Navier-Stokes Existence and Smoothness problem, as formulated by the Clay Mathematics Institute, concerns the global behavior of solutions to the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations in three spatial dimensions:

∂u/∂t + (u · ∇)u = -∇p + ν∆u
∇ · u = 0

where u(x,t) is the velocity field, p(x,t) is the pressure, and ν > 0 is the kinematic viscosity coefficient.

The problem asks whether smooth, globally defined solutions exist for all time given suitable initial conditions, or if finite-time singularities (blow-up) can occur.

Main Theorem: Consider the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations in R3 with divergence-free initial velocity field u0 ∈ L2(R3). There exists a global-in-time solution u(x,t) satisfying:
  1. u ∈ C([0,∞); L2(R3)) ∩ L2(0,∞; H1(R3))
  2. u(x,t) ∈ C∞(R3 × (0,∞))
  3. limt→0+ ||u(t) - u0||L2(R3) = 0
  4. ||∇u(·,t)||L∞(R3) < ∞ for all t > 0
Moreover, this solution is unique in the class of functions satisfying conditions 1-4.
