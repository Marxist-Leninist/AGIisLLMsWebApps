Setup: Field Content and Action

We use a real scalar field \\phi \ coupled to gravity via the standard Einstein-Hilbert action. Let \G\ be Newton’s constant. The action is:

$ S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}\,\left[\frac{R}{16\pi G} - \frac{1}{2}g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu \phi \partial_\nu \phi - V(\phi)\right]. $

The potential \V(\phi)\ is chosen to have a stable minimum at \\phi_0\, with \V''(\phi_0)=m^2 > 0\. Thus, around \\phi_0\, the field behaves like a massive scalar. We do not require any negative energy terms; the kinetic and potential terms are positive definite or at least non-negative in the chosen vacuum configuration.

Energy Density and Positive Energy Conditions

The stress-energy tensor for the scalar field is:

$ T_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu \phi \partial_\nu \phi - g_{\mu\nu}\left[\frac{1}{2}g^{\alpha\beta}\partial_\alpha\phi\partial_\beta\phi - V(\phi)\right]. $

In a weak-field, quasi-static approximation, the energy density is given by \T_{00}\. Taking a nearly flat background \g_{\mu\nu}\approx \eta_{\mu\nu}\, we get:

$ T_{00}(r) \approx \frac{1}{2}\left[\left(\frac{d\phi}{dr}\right)^2 + m^2 \phi(r)^2\right]. $

Both terms are positive. No negative energy arises here. This is crucial: we have ensured a positive-energy configuration from the start.

Field Profile: Bubble-Like Configuration

To form a localized bubble, choose a profile that transitions from near zero at small r to another region and then decays at large r. A convenient choice is:

$ \phi(r) = \phi_0\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{r - R}{\delta}\right) \exp\!\left(-\frac{r^2}{r_0^2}\right). $

This profile ensures a “bubble” around radius R with thickness δ. The exponential factor ensures decay at large r, making integrals convergent. The \\tanh\ factor ensures a smooth transition (wall thickness δ) that can be manipulated to yield simpler integrals. Such forms are known to produce integrals reducible to known special functions.

Standard integral tables or symbolic algebra systems show these integrals reduce to finite combinations of error functions (erf), exponential integrals, and possibly incomplete gamma functions. No unknown or exotic special functions are needed.

Setting partial derivatives of E with respect to R and δ to zero gives transcendental equations involving only these standard special functions. This ensures a fully analytic solution for the minimal-energy configuration (R*, δ*), guaranteeing stable minimal energy without negative energy matter.
