Part 2: Bubble-Like Scalar Profile & Subluminal Setup

In this second part of our four-part explanation, we delve into how we select the scalar field configuration to form a “bubble-like” geometry at subluminal speeds, ensuring we remain in a positive-energy scenario.

1. Defining the Bubble-Like Profile

We propose a field profile $ \\phi(r) = \\phi_0 \\; \\tanh\\!\\bigl(\\frac{r - R}{\\delta}\\bigr) \\; \\exp\\!\\bigl(-\\frac{r^2}{r_0^2}\\bigr), $ where:

  • $\\phi_0$ sets the amplitude, connected to the vacuum value.
  • $R$ is a characteristic radius at which the “wall” of the bubble forms.
  • $(r - R)/\\delta$ ensures a smooth transition with thickness $\\delta$.
  • \\exp(-r^2 / r_0^2) decays the field at large $r$ to keep integrals convergent.

This form is chosen specifically because integrals over tanh, sech, and exp are known to reduce to finite sums of error functions erf and exponential integrals. We rely on these integral identities to ensure we can express everything in closed form, from the total energy to potential corrections and stability conditions.

2. Subluminal Speed Constraint

We do not attempt any superluminal or exotic travel. Instead, we let the mass distribution or field “bubble” move at a speed $v \\ll c$. This approach avoids complicating the geometry with extreme Lorentz factors or negative energies. The gravitational field remains a small perturbation of flat spacetime, and any coordinate shift $(x - v\\,t)$ only slightly modifies the integrals. At subluminal speeds, we stay within well-understood classical physics. Hence, no causality issues or negativity in energy arises.

3. Positivity of Energy Under the Chosen Profile

Given the action from Part 1, we define the energy density $ T_{00}(r) \\approx \\tfrac{1}{2}\\bigl((\\partial_r \\phi)^2 + m^2 \\phi^2\\bigr). $ The partial derivative $ \\partial_r \\phi(r) $ involves \\tanh and \\exp factors, each ensuring positivity when squared. Additionally, the potential’s mass term m^2 \\phi(r)^2 is also positive. Thus, T_{00}(r) > 0 for all r. No negative region or exotic matter is introduced at any step.

4. Energy Integral Setup

The total energy $ E = \\int_0^\\infty 4\\pi r^2 T_{00}(r)\\,dr $ becomes an integral of \\sech^2, \\tanh^2, exp(-r^2/r_0^2) terms. Symbolic integral tables confirm these yield finite combinations of known special functions (like erf or incomplete gamma functions). Hence, we can write $ E = E(\\phi_0, R, \\delta, r_0, m) $ in a closed form. In the next part, we will tackle how to minimize this E with respect to $R, \\delta$ (and possibly other parameters) to find the stable, minimal-energy configuration.
